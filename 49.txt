The OA should specify the member's allocation of equity, assets, income and loss, and of course - managerial powers and signature authorities. In your case - it should say that the LLC is single-member entity and the single member has all the managerial powers and authorities - what is called \"member-managed\". Every LLC is required to have an operating agreement, although you don't necessarily have to file it with the State or record it. If you don't have your own OA, default rules will apply, depending on your State law. However, the bank will probably not take you as a customer without an explicit OA.
A single member limited liability company (LLC) is a type of business structure in which there is only one owner, who is also the sole member of the LLC. This means that the owner has complete control over the company and is personally responsible for all of its debts and obligations.In terms of the breakdown of ownership, a single member LLC is owned 100% by the single member, who is also the sole owner of the company. The member may choose to transfer ownership of the LLC to another individual or entity through a sale or gift, but the member will always retain full control until such a transfer takes place.It's important to note that the member of a single member LLC is still subject to the same legal protections as any other member of an LLC. This means that the member is not personally liable for the debts and obligations of the LLC, and their personal assets are protected from legal action taken against the company.If the financial institution you are working with has requested a breakdown of ownership for a single member LLC, they may be looking for information on the ownership structure of the company and the percentage of ownership held by the member. In this case, you can simply provide them with the information that the member owns 100% of the company.
